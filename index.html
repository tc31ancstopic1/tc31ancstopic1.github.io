What is a gyroscope?

Working principle of Gyroscope

What are gyroscopes and how does it work?

The Gyroscope are rotating disc that would change direction when force is applied. The Gyroscope consist of the spin axis, rotor and the gimbal. It follows the laws of angular momentum L = Iω where L = angular momentum(Vector) , I = Moment of Inertia and ω = angular velocity(Vector). When force is applied, the direction and angular momentum will change. To find the direction of angular momentum, the right hand rule is used. The right hand rule states that both angular momentum L and angular velocity are perpendicular to the direction of rotation. When external force is applied to a rotating disc, an output torque τ will be produced and it will always be perpendicular to the angular momentum L. To counter this effect , 2 Gyroscope will be used and each will be placed perpendicular to one another so that the torque τ can be counteracted

Flight instrumentation and avionics system onboard the commercial aircraft

What are flight instruments and what are they used for?

The instruments present on a commercial aircraft is an essential for flights. It is mandatory for all pilots to learn how to understand and use the instruments in order to ensure a safe flight. These instruments can tell the pilots on information regarding the aircraft such as how fast the aircraft is travelling to alerting the pilots on any hazards such as thunderstorms ahead.

There are many instruments in an aircraft, each instrument will provide different information to the pilots. The systems in the aircraft can be classified mainly into 3 categories which are communication, Navigation and the Cockpit Display System.

Benefits of using gyroscope in flight instrumentation and avionics systems

Gyroscopes helps with awareness

As part of the avionics instruments. The gyroscope is very important instrument which provides the attitude, compass, vertical speed indicator and the turn coordinates. It also allows precise and safe trespass through the clouds. Normally for pilots that flies under VMC(Visual Meteorological Conditions) normally relies on the gyroscope to get get out of IMC(Instrument meteorological conditions) situations.


General aviation has long enough been using gyro devices to get the bugs figured out pretty well. See on the ground how the instruments respond. Those showing motion on the yaw axis should shift freely during the taxi, and any changes in pitch should be seen by the AI, such as crossing the potholes in front of the Fixes Based Operator(FBO). If you hear one of the gyros moaning over the engine's roar, it's a good bet that this planet won't be long for the instrument. For the pneumatic instruments, also be alert for proper suction indications; the gauge should be not well above idle velocity in the green arc and most certainly by the run-up rpm. As well as one below the green arc, watch for too high an indication; too much suction will spin the gyros past their design limits.

Challenges of using gyroscopes in the flight instrumentation and avionics systems

What are some of the challenges when using gyrosscopic equipment

One of the few challenges when using a gyroscopic equipment includes getting error readings during flights. When there are complications such as: high altitude, choked filter, suction tube system leak etc., two abnormalities or errors will occur

  • Lowered gyroscope speed

    • The rate of turn will be under-read and the rigidity of the gyroscope is lowered. The aircraft is turning faster than expected by pilot due to the erroneous reading.

  • Higher gyroscope speed

    • The rate of turn is over-read as the gyroscope rigidity is increased. The aircraft is turning slower than expected by pilot due to the erroneous reading.

Artificial horizon readings are dependant on the gyroscope, more importantly in its control systems to ensure that the rotational axis remains vertical.

  • Gyroscope affected by acceleraration

    • Due to its mass, the gyroscope can be affected by acceleration. When an aircraft accelerates during take off, nose-up and right hand turn indications may be incorrect.

  • Errors in readings

    • During deceleration, nose-down and left turn indications may be incorrect.

  • Over or under-reading of bank angles

    • During aircraft turning, there is a slight acceleration to the centre of the turn due to centripetal force. The artificial horizon will either over-read or under-read the bank angle by approximately 20 degrees during the turn.

  • Limited movement range

    • The movement of the instrument case in a gyroscope is limited and is controlled by fixed stops. Once it has hit the limits of the pitch and roll, the fixed stops acts as a force against the gyroscope. These causes erratic indications on the artificial horizon until the gyroscope realigns itself, which can take about 10-15 minutes to rectify.

Direction Gyro Indicator is the most sensitive to any abnormalities or incorrect readings among all the other gyroscopic flight instruments. However, they might still be susceptible to errors during flight.

  • Wear and tear

    • Due to friction, certain manufacturing defects and overall wear and tear, the quality of the flight instrument can degrade over time which can lead to a misalignment of the gyroscope.

  • Earth rotation error

    • The rotor axis of a gyroscope remains in position while the Earth rotates. This causes the observer to perceive that the gyroscope has changed position. This error is dependant on the latitude, at 15°/hour on both poles.

  • Change in latitude

    • During flight, there is a change in latitude. It may be rectified on its own due to Earth’s rotation, but after many flights over time it needs to be corrected again.

  • Limited instrument case movement

    • Similar to that of the artificial horizon, the movement of the instrument case is limited and controlled by fixed stops. The fixed stops acts as a force against the gyroscope when the limits are reached. These causes erratic indications on the instrument until the gyroscope realigns itself.
